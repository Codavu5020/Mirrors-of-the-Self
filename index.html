Section III · Project Essay

The Courage to Look Inward

"Know thyself" — two words that have outlasted empires, yet feel as urgent now as they did in ancient Athens.

We live in an age of noise. Notifications fight for our attention, social feeds curate our self-image, and the pace of life rarely leaves room for the simple, necessary act of sitting still with oneself. Mirrors of the Soul is my attempt to do exactly that — to hold up a mirror, however uncomfortable, and truly look.

This final requirement is not merely an academic exercise. It is a record of a semester spent turning over the many-sided gem that is human identity. Through Eastern and Western philosophy, through psychology and anthropology, through the body, the digital self, and the spiritual life — I have encountered frameworks that each illuminate a different facet of what it means to be a person.

The title Mirrors of the Soul captures what I believe self-understanding truly is: a collection of reflections. No single mirror shows the whole picture. It is only when we gather Eastern introspection alongside Western individualism, biological reality alongside social construction, material existence alongside spiritual yearning, that a fuller, richer image of the self begins to emerge.

What follows is that collection of reflections — honest, imperfect, and very much still in progress.

Section V · Module 1 Post-Assessment

Cultural Influences in IT Learning

Module 1 · Infographic
🔍 Click to enlarge

I cannot pinpoint a single person who influenced me to pursue a career in IT. Instead, it was the tech community that shaped my interest in this field. A specific moment that stands out was when I was trying to fix a coding error by searching GitHub and Stack Overflow, where other programmers explain possible solutions. This community reflects both Eastern and Western characteristics.

🌸 Eastern Perspective

There is a strong sense of camaraderie and shared responsibility in helping each other learn.

🏛 Western Perspective

The community encourages independence, innovation, and openness to sharing new ideas.

I encountered cultural differences during group projects as teammates also had different communication styles. I learned to be open-minded and respectful, which improved teamwork. As an IT student, one concrete action I can take is to ensure equal participation during team meetings by actively inviting quieter members to share their ideas. In the future, I can design software with inclusive and user-friendly features for people from different cultural backgrounds.

Thinking & Feeling

Module 2 · Philosophical & Psychological
🔍 Click to enlarge

There was a moment in my life where I realized that my identity was shaped by external expectations rather than my values. This experience is what relates me to William James' concept of Me-Self and I-Self. My Me-Self was influenced by feedback from others while my I-Self chose to reflect and learn from those experiences. My personal philosophy would be "I am not defined by my circumstances, but by how I respond to them." This philosophy also aligns with Albert Bandura's ideas about personal responsibility and choice.

💭 Present and Future

Dear Future Self, today I am still exploring the unknown — reflective and motivated, though sometimes uncertain. According to Erikson's Theory of Identity versus Role Confusion, this stage of questioning is necessary for growth. Ten years from now, I hope you remember to stay true to yourself, never stop learning, and make choices that reflect the real you.

🧩 Ideal Self

My real self and ideal self differ in consistency but share the same values. While I strive to improve in my craft, I sometimes fall short of my own expectations. I practice self-acceptance instead of self-criticism — little by little I am reducing that gap. This reflects Carl Jung's Idea of Self as Wholeness and Sigmund Freud's View of Inner Conflict, helping me better understand myself.

Section IX · Material and Digital Self Post-Assessment

Material Self & Online Facade

Module 5 · Material & Digital Self
🔍 Click to enlarge

My identity is shaped by both what I have and how I present myself online.

🏠 Material Self

I value simple, comfortable clothing that reflects my true personality rather than trying to impress. My family is the most important part of my world, giving me support and a sense of belonging. Among my possessions, my phone is essential — it connects me to people and information.

💻 Digital Self

Online, I present myself as positive and thoughtful, but not completely open. I tend to share only the good parts of my life, which means my digital self is real but somewhat filtered. I sometimes feel pressure from comparison and the need for validation, so I remind myself to stay respectful and true to my values. I aim to be more genuine so my online presence better reflects who I truly am.

Section X · Spiritual & Political Self Post-Assessment

Faith, Power & Purpose

Module 6 · Spiritual & Political Self
🔍 Click to enlarge

The deepest questions of self inevitably lead us to transcendence and to power. The spiritual self asks: Is there something greater than me? What gives my life ultimate meaning? The political self asks: How do I relate to power, justice, and the collective? Both dimensions share a common root — how we locate ourselves in relation to something larger than our individual existence.

🕊 Spiritual Self

My spiritual self is rooted in faith, inner peace, and compassion, which give meaning and direction to my life. It guides me to choose what is right and honest, even in difficult situations. My spiritual identity has been shaped by my family, religious community, and personal experiences that deepened my beliefs, providing comfort, strength, and hope.

🗳 Political Self

My political self is moderate and open-minded, guided by values of fairness, accountability, and inclusivity. School, media, and peer discussions have influenced my views by teaching me critical thinking and exposing me to different perspectives. Over time, my beliefs have shifted from being family-influenced to becoming more independent through education and personal reflection.

Section XI · Parting Words

A Letter to My Future Self

"The unexamined life is not worth living." — And perhaps the examined life is the most courageous thing we can attempt.

This semester helped me understand some things about myself. I discovered that growth only begins in discomfort. Reflections showed me that courage is not loud — it is the quiet persistence in facing what I would rather avoid.

Collaboration reveals both my strengths and weaknesses. There were moments when I admitted I didn't have all the answers; reflecting on the lessons made me realize the uncomfortable truth. I'm still learning to balance confidence with humility, and that remains a work in progress.

I will carry the idea that self-examination is a lifelong journey. Reflecting by questioning, observing, and adjusting will guide me on how to approach both technology and relationships. Once in a while I'll slow down, stay curious, and keep learning with intention.

To my future self: I promise to keep looking in the mirror — not with judgment, but with curiosity and compassion. I promise to stay a student of my own life. And I promise that the self I am today is not the ceiling, but the foundation.
